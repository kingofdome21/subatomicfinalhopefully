ELECTRON

e-

Mass: 0.511 MeV/c²

Charge: -1 e

  1. Electrons are fundamental particles that orbit around the nucleus of an atom.
  2. They have a negative electric charge and are much lighter than protons or neutrons.
  3. Electrons are responsible for chemical bonding between atoms.
  4. They were discovered by J.J. Thomson in 1897.
  5. Electrons can behave as both particles and waves, demonstrating quantum properties.

NUCLEUS

p+ n

Components: Protons and Neutrons

Charge: Positive (from protons)

  1. The nucleus is the dense central region of an atom.
  2. It contains protons (positively charged) and neutrons (neutral).
  3. The nucleus makes up less than 0.01% of an atom's volume but contains over 99.9% of its mass.
  4. It was discovered by Ernest Rutherford in 1911.
  5. The strong nuclear force holds the nucleus together, overcoming the electromagnetic repulsion between protons.

PROTON

p+

Mass: 938.27 MeV/c²

Charge: +1 e

Composition: 2 up quarks, 1 down quark

  1. A proton is the positively charged particle that, together with the electrically neutral particles called neutrons, make up the nucleus of an atom.
  2. It has a positive electric charge equal in magnitude to that of an electron.
  3. Its mass is slightly less than the mass of a neutron and 1,836 times the mass of an electron.
  4. Protons and neutrons, each with masses of approximately one atomic mass unit, are jointly referred to as "nucleons" (particles present in atomic nuclei).
  5. One or more protons are present in the nucleus of every atom. They provide the attractive electrostatic central force which binds the atomic electrons.

NEUTRON

n

Mass: 939.57 MeV/c²

Charge: 0 e

Composition: 1 up quark, 2 down quarks

  1. A neutron is a subatomic particle with no electric charge, found in the nucleus of every atom except hydrogen-1.
  2. Neutrons and protons make up the nucleus of an atom, and are collectively called nucleons.
  3. The neutron has a mass slightly greater than that of a proton and about 1,839 times the mass of an electron.
  4. Outside the nucleus, free neutrons are unstable and have a mean lifetime of about 15 minutes, decaying into a proton, an electron, and an antineutrino.
  5. Neutrons play an important role in nuclear fission and fusion reactions.

UP QUARK

u

Mass: 2.2 MeV/c²

Charge: +⅔ e

  1. The up quark is the lightest of all quarks.
  2. It has a fractional electric charge of +⅔.
  3. Up quarks are found in protons (2 up quarks, 1 down quark) and neutrons (1 up quark, 2 down quarks).
  4. It is a first-generation quark, meaning it's one of the building blocks of ordinary matter.
  5. Up quarks are never found in isolation due to a phenomenon called color confinement.

FUNDAMENTAL PARTICLES

A quark is a type of elementary particle and a fundamental constituent of matter. Quarks combine to form composite particles called hadrons, the most stable of which are protons and neutrons, the components of atomic nuclei. All commonly observable matter is composed of up quarks, down quarks and electrons. Due to a phenomenon known as colour confinement, quarks are never found in isolation; they can be found only within hadrons, which include baryons (such as protons and neutrons) and mesons, or in quark–gluon plasmas.

STRANGE QUARK

s

Mass: 95 MeV/c²

Charge: -⅓ e

  1. The strange quark is a second-generation quark.
  2. It has a fractional electric charge of -⅓, the same as the down quark.
  3. Strange quarks are found in exotic hadrons like kaons and some hyperons.
  4. It was named "strange" because particles containing it lived longer than expected.
  5. Strange quarks are produced in high-energy collisions but quickly decay to lighter quarks.

CHARM QUARK

c

Mass: 1.28 GeV/c²

Charge: +⅔ e

  1. The charm quark is a second-generation quark.
  2. It has a fractional electric charge of +⅔, the same as the up quark.
  3. Charm quarks are found in particles like the J/ψ meson and D mesons.
  4. It was discovered simultaneously by two independent research groups in 1974.
  5. The discovery of the charm quark provided strong evidence for the quark model of particle physics.

BOTTOM QUARK

b

Mass: 4.18 GeV/c²

Charge: -⅓ e

  1. The bottom quark (also called beauty quark) is a third-generation quark.
  2. It has a fractional electric charge of -⅓, like the down and strange quarks.
  3. Bottom quarks are found in B mesons and other exotic particles.
  4. It was discovered in 1977 at Fermilab.
  5. The study of bottom quarks has provided important insights into CP violation and matter-antimatter asymmetry.

TOP QUARK

t

Mass: 173.1 GeV/c²

Charge: +⅔ e

  1. The top quark is the most massive of all known elementary particles.
  2. It is a third-generation quark with a charge of +⅔ e.
  3. It was discovered in 1995 at Fermilab.
  4. The top quark decays extremely quickly, before it can form hadrons.
  5. Its large mass makes it important for studying the Higgs boson and electroweak symmetry breaking.

ELECTRON

e-

Mass: 0.511 MeV/c²

Charge: -1 e

  1. The electron is a fundamental particle that orbits around the nucleus of an atom.
  2. It has a negative electric charge and is much lighter than protons or neutrons.
  3. Electrons are responsible for chemical bonding between atoms.
  4. They were discovered by J.J. Thomson in 1897.
  5. Electrons can behave as both particles and waves, demonstrating quantum properties.

MUON

μ-

Mass: 105.7 MeV/c²

Charge: -1 e

  1. The muon is a second-generation lepton, similar to the electron but about 207 times heavier.
  2. It has a negative electric charge equal to that of an electron.
  3. Muons are unstable and decay with a mean lifetime of about 2.2 microseconds.
  4. They were discovered in cosmic rays in 1936.
  5. Muons can penetrate matter much more deeply than electrons due to their higher mass.

TAU

τ-

Mass: 1,777 MeV/c²

Charge: -1 e

  1. The tau is a third-generation lepton, similar to the electron and muon but much heavier.
  2. It has a negative electric charge equal to that of an electron.
  3. Tau particles are extremely unstable with a mean lifetime of only 2.9×10^-13 seconds.
  4. They were discovered in 1975 at the SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory.
  5. Tau particles can decay into hadrons as well as lighter leptons.

ELECTRON NEUTRINO

νe

Mass: < 2 eV/c² (exact value unknown)

Charge: 0

  1. The electron neutrino is a first-generation lepton associated with the electron.
  2. It has no electric charge and very little mass, making it extremely difficult to detect.
  3. Electron neutrinos are produced in beta decay and nuclear fusion reactions.
  4. They were first theorized by Wolfgang Pauli in 1930 and detected in 1956.
  5. Neutrinos can pass through ordinary matter with almost no interaction.

MUON NEUTRINO

νμ

Mass: < 2 eV/c² (exact value unknown)

Charge: 0

  1. The muon neutrino is a second-generation lepton associated with the muon.
  2. It has no electric charge and very little mass.
  3. Muon neutrinos are produced in the decay of pions and muons.
  4. They were first detected in 1962 at Brookhaven National Laboratory.
  5. Like all neutrinos, they interact very weakly with matter.

TAU NEUTRINO

ντ

Mass: < 2 eV/c² (exact value unknown)

Charge: 0

  1. The tau neutrino is a third-generation lepton associated with the tau particle.
  2. It has no electric charge and very little mass.
  3. Tau neutrinos are produced in the decay of tau leptons.
  4. They were the last of the fundamental fermions to be directly observed, detected in 2000 at Fermilab.
  5. Like other neutrinos, they interact extremely weakly with matter.

PHOTON

γ

Mass: 0

Charge: 0

  1. The photon is the quantum of the electromagnetic field and the force carrier for electromagnetic force.
  2. It is massless and always travels at the speed of light in vacuum.
  3. Photons are the particles that make up light and all other forms of electromagnetic radiation.
  4. The concept was developed by Albert Einstein to explain the photoelectric effect.
  5. Photons exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties (wave-particle duality).

GLUON

g

Mass: 0

Charge: 0

  1. Gluons are the force carriers of the strong nuclear force that binds quarks together.
  2. They are massless and carry a property called "color charge."
  3. Unlike photons, gluons can interact with each other due to their color charge.
  4. There are eight different types of gluons.
  5. Gluons were first detected indirectly in three-jet events at DESY in 1979.

W BOSON

W±

Mass: 80.4 GeV/c²

Charge: ±1 e

  1. W bosons are the force carriers of the weak nuclear force.
  2. They come in two charged varieties: W+ and W-.
  3. W bosons are responsible for nuclear beta decay and other weak interactions.
  4. They were discovered in 1983 at CERN.
  5. Unlike photons and gluons, W bosons are massive particles.

Z BOSON

Z0

Mass: 91.2 GeV/c²

Charge: 0

  1. The Z boson is a neutral force carrier of the weak nuclear force.
  2. It mediates neutral current interactions in the weak force.
  3. Z bosons were discovered in 1983 at CERN, shortly after the W bosons.
  4. They are much heavier than protons or neutrons.
  5. The Z boson's properties have been precisely measured at particle colliders.

HIGGS BOSON

H0

Mass: 125.25 GeV/c²

Charge: 0

  1. The Higgs boson is associated with the Higgs field, which gives mass to other elementary particles.
  2. It was the last particle predicted by the Standard Model to be discovered.
  3. The Higgs boson was discovered in 2012 at CERN's Large Hadron Collider.
  4. Its discovery confirmed the existence of the Higgs field and completed the Standard Model.
  5. The Higgs mechanism explains why some force carriers have mass while others don't.
